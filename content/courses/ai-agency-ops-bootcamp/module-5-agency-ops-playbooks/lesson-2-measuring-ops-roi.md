# Measuring Ops ROI

You've built a lot of workflows across the first four modules. This lesson is about proving that they're worth the time and effort. Without measurement, AI ops work quietly dies when a busy week hits.

## The three numbers
Measure three things, monthly.

**Hours saved.** Compare how long a process used to take versus how long it takes now with AI. Multiply by frequency. Sum across workflows. This is your raw time reclaimed.

**Output quality.** Did the automated or AI-assisted outputs hold up to the standard you had before? Pick a sample each month and review quality. If quality drifted, the workflow needs tuning. Quality is the counterweight to speed; don't let one kill the other.

**Revenue touched.** For workflows that interact with clients directly (retention outreach, cross-sell triggers, new lead triage), track the dollar value of the policies that touched the workflow in a given month. This is the revenue that is exposed to the system.

Three numbers, one spreadsheet, fifteen minutes per month to update.

## Baseline first
You cannot measure hours saved without a baseline. Before you roll out a workflow, time yourself doing the manual version. Write that number down.

This sounds obvious but most agencies skip it. Then three months later someone asks "so how much time did the AI save us?" and nobody has any data to answer with. Don't be that agency. Spend ten minutes creating a baseline before you automate anything.

## Quality is hard to measure, do it anyway
The quality metric is the slipperiest of the three. What does a high-quality AI-drafted email even look like?

Here is a practical definition. A high-quality output is one that, when a human reviews it before sending, requires less than 60 seconds of editing. A medium-quality output requires 60 to 180 seconds. A low-quality output takes more than three minutes or needs a full rewrite.

Sample ten outputs from each workflow every month. Time yourself reviewing them. Log whether each was high, medium, or low. If low outputs creep above 20 percent, something in your prompt or your inputs needs attention.

## Revenue touched is not revenue attributed
Be careful with the revenue touched metric. If $60,000 of renewal premium ran through your retention workflow this month, that does not mean the workflow was responsible for retaining all of it. Some would have retained anyway.

The honest way to talk about revenue touched is "the system is exposed to X dollars of revenue each month, and our retention rate has moved from Y to Z since we started." That framing is defensible. "The system made us $60,000" is not.

## Monthly review ritual
Block 30 minutes on the last Friday of every month. Pull the three numbers for the month. Write down what changed, what you are going to adjust, and one thing you are going to try next month.

Over a year, that ritual turns into a clear story of what worked and what didn't. You will know which workflows to double down on and which to quietly retire. The data is more valuable than the automation itself.

**Do this today:** Open a fresh spreadsheet. Create one row per workflow you are running. Three columns: hours saved this month, quality sample result, revenue touched. That is your ops scorecard.
