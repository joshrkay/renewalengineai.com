# Rolling Out to Producers and CSRs

You have the workflows. You have the SOP library. You have the measurement system. None of it matters if your team does not actually use any of it. This lesson is about the rollout conversation and the first few weeks that determine whether the whole system takes root.

## Why rollouts fail
Most AI rollouts in small agencies fail for the same two reasons.

One, the owner builds the system in isolation and then drops it on the team with a "here's how we do things now" email. The team feels imposed on, finds one reason the system doesn't fit their workflow, and quietly stops using it.

Two, the owner tries to change too much at once. Four new workflows, two new tools, a new dashboard, and a new SOP library all land in the same week. The team panics and defaults back to the old way.

Both failures are prevented by the same principle: slow, deliberate, together.

## The first conversation
Before you build the system (or after, if you already have), have a 45-minute sit-down with every team member who will be affected. Show them the current state, the pain points you are trying to fix, the specific outcomes you want (hours saved, fewer dropped balls, more time for high-value work), and the specific workflows you are proposing.

Then, and this is the critical part, ask for their input. What would break in their workflow if you did this? What tool that they already use should be part of the system? What are they most worried about?

Listen. Adjust the plan based on what you hear. When they see their input in the final version, they own the rollout with you instead of against you.

## The first two weeks
Pick exactly one workflow to roll out first. Not three. One.

Week one is training. Walk through the workflow with the team. Demonstrate it. Have each team member do it themselves with you watching, in both directions: they watch you, then you watch them.

Week two is supervised running. They use the workflow on real work. You check in at the end of each day to see what's working and what's not. Tweak the prompts and steps based on what you learn.

By the end of week two, the workflow either works for the team or it doesn't. If it works, add a second workflow in week three. If it doesn't, spend week three fixing it, not adding more.

## The two things that kill adoption
Two things kill adoption faster than anything else.

**Hidden failures.** An AI draft that had a bad hallucination and embarrassed a team member with a client, never discussed openly. The team member loses trust, stops using the workflow, and doesn't tell you why. Prevent this by creating a no-blame failure log. If something breaks, it goes in the log, gets discussed, and gets fixed.

**Silent resistance.** A team member who thinks this is going to replace their job and quietly sabotages the rollout. Prevent this by being explicit: the goal is to remove the boring work from their day so they can focus on the interesting work, not to shrink the team.

## After rollout
Six months after a successful rollout, most agencies find that they are doing things they literally could not do before. Faster quote comparisons, tighter response SLAs, more proactive check-ins, a real SOP library, and measurable time savings.

The team also notices something subtle: they enjoy the work more. The tedious parts are gone. The remaining work is closer to the craft of being an agent. That is the actual reward for all of this effort, and it is the reason the team will fight to keep the system running once they have it.

**Do this today:** Schedule that first 45-minute conversation. Block it on your calendar. Invite the people who will be affected. Do not cancel it.
