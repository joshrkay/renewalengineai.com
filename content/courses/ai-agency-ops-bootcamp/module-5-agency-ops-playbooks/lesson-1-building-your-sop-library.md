# Building Your SOP Library

Every agency has procedures that live in exactly one place: the owner's head. A few of the simpler tasks might have a half-written document on a shared drive from 2019 that nobody has opened since. The commercial binding process? In the owner's head. The COI escalation rules? In the senior CSR's head. The referral handling protocol? Nobody is entirely sure there is one. The knowledge works when you have one employee. It breaks when you have three. It catastrophically breaks when you try to take a real two-week vacation or the senior CSR leaves for a better job and takes ten years of tribal knowledge out the door with her.

Most agencies have tried to build SOPs before and given up because writing them from scratch is slow, tedious, and feels lower-priority than every other thing on the schedule. The last attempt produced three half-documents, one of them is accurate, two of them are wrong, nobody remembers which is which. The whole effort quietly died.

AI changes the economics of SOP writing enough that the whole project becomes viable again. A task that used to take 90 minutes of sitting down with a blank document and trying to remember every step now takes 10 to 15 minutes of conversational narration to a chat AI that structures the output for you. That shift — from "this will take an afternoon" to "this will take ten minutes" — is the difference between the SOPs getting written and staying in your head. This lesson shows you the co-author approach, the ten SOPs to write first, the storage discipline that keeps the library alive, and the quarterly review ritual that prevents drift.

By the end of this lesson you will have a working AI co-author prompt, a prioritized list of the ten SOPs most agencies should build first, a storage and ownership structure, and a review cadence that keeps the library honest over the next two years.

## Why SOP libraries usually fail

Three failure modes show up in almost every attempt before this one. Recognize each of them so you do not repeat them.

**Failure 1 — Writing SOPs the owner already "kind of" has.** Somebody decides to formalize the agency's procedures. They open a blank doc. They try to describe how new business intake works. The description immediately gets stuck on the edge cases ("well, if it's commercial we do X, but if it's above $500k premium we do Y, unless it's a carrier we don't have an appetite with, in which case..."). The description is 40 percent done, 60 percent edge cases, and the author gives up after two hours. The draft goes in a folder. Nobody looks at it again.

**Failure 2 — Treating SOPs as a documentation project, not an operational tool.** The team spends weeks formatting SOPs into a polished wiki with headers, screenshots, navigation menus, and beautiful fonts. The actual content is 70 percent accurate. Nobody uses it because it was never about the content — it was about the appearance of professionalism. The library becomes a showpiece, not a tool.

**Failure 3 — Writing once and never updating.** The SOP was accurate on the day it was written. Two months later the carrier portal changed. Four months later the referral process got modified. A year later the SOP describes a process that no longer exists. New CSRs follow the obsolete SOP, get confused, ask questions, stop using the library, and you are back to tribal knowledge with extra documentation overhead on top.

AI helps with Failure 1 (it catches the gaps and prompts you for the edge cases) and Failure 2 (the output is functional, not cosmetic). Failure 3 is solved by the quarterly review ritual at the end of this lesson.

## The AI co-author approach

The approach works like this. You pick a process. You open Claude or ChatGPT in a fresh conversation. You paste the SOP prompt (below). You then narrate, out loud or in text, how you actually do the task — in whatever order things come to mind, with all the messy edge cases and "it depends" moments included. The AI drafts a structured SOP from your narration, asks clarifying questions for the parts you skipped, and returns a clean document. You read it, correct any mistakes, and save it.

Total time per SOP: 10 to 15 minutes for a process you already know well, 20 to 30 for a process you half-know and have to think through as you describe it.

Here is the co-author prompt. Paste this at the start of each SOP session.

> I am going to describe an agency process to you. Your job is to listen, ask clarifying questions for any gaps, and then produce a structured SOP document with exactly these sections:
>
> 1. **Purpose** — one sentence stating what this SOP is for and why it exists.
> 2. **Trigger** — what event, request, or condition starts this process.
> 3. **Inputs** — what data, documents, or tools are needed before starting.
> 4. **Step-by-step procedure** — numbered steps in order, each step one or two sentences, concrete and actionable. Include the specific tool or location where the step happens.
> 5. **Expected output** — what should exist or be different after the process is complete.
> 6. **Quality check** — how to verify the output is correct.
> 7. **Escalation path** — what to do when the process does not work as described, and who to escalate to.
> 8. **Last updated** — insert today's date.
>
> Rules:
> - Use plain language. No jargon that a new CSR would not understand.
> - Do not invent steps I did not describe. If a step seems missing, ask me about it instead of guessing.
> - Flag any part of my description that is ambiguous or contradictory.
> - Keep the full SOP under 600 words.
>
> Ready for my narration of the first process.

Then just talk. Describe how you handle a COI request start to finish. Or how you onboard a new commercial client from signed application to bound policy. Or how you close out a claim and do the post-claim follow-up. The AI catches the order, asks about gaps ("you mentioned checking the appetite — where exactly do you check that?"), and returns a clean structured document.

The questions the AI asks are the secret weapon. Half the value of the exercise is not the document — it is the questions that surface the parts of your process you are doing unconsciously and have never articulated. "You said you review the loss runs before quoting — what specifically are you looking for?" is the kind of question that turns a tribal-knowledge agency into a documented one.

## The ten SOPs to write first

You do not need 50 SOPs. You need 10 that cover 90 percent of repeated work. Most agencies need almost the same 10. Here is the starter list, in rough priority order.

1. **New client onboarding** — from first contact through welcome kit delivery.
2. **New policy binding** — from "quote accepted" through policy issued and welcome email sent.
3. **Renewal outreach sequence** — the trigger-based cadence from the Retention course.
4. **Claim intake** — what happens when a client reports a claim for the first time.
5. **Claim follow-up** — post-claim check-in routine and how to handle open escalations.
6. **COI request handling** — the workflow from Lesson 4.2.
7. **Endorsement request handling** — the sibling workflow to COIs.
8. **Lead intake and routing** — the classification and SLA process from Module 2.
9. **Cross-sell opportunity handling** — what to do when a client surfaces a need for additional coverage.
10. **Referral handling** — what to do with an inbound referral, including thank-you cadence to the referrer.

Draft all ten in a single week with AI. Spend 30 minutes a day for five days, two SOPs per session. Total elapsed time: about 2 to 3 hours for the whole library. Without AI this same project would take most agency owners several weekends and would almost certainly not finish.

You do not need to get all 10 perfect on the first pass. You need them to be 80 percent accurate. The missing 20 percent gets fixed the first time the SOP runs into reality, which is why the quarterly review exists.

## Storage and access

Store your SOPs somewhere searchable. Any team member should be able to find any SOP in under 30 seconds. If they cannot, nobody uses the library and you are back to tribal knowledge plus the false impression of documentation.

Three good options, in rough order of fit:

- **Notion** — free or cheap, great search, easy to link between SOPs, easy to tag by process area. Best fit for most small agencies. The learning curve is real but the 1-hour investment pays back forever.
- **Google Drive with a consistent naming convention** — free, familiar to everyone, search works if you name files consistently (`sop-new-policy-binding.md`). Less pretty than Notion but zero learning curve.
- **A simple wiki tool like Obsidian or Bookstack** — better for agencies with a technical owner who enjoys that tooling. Skip if nobody on the team already uses one of these.

Do not use Word documents on a shared drive. Do not use emailed attachments. Do not use a tool nobody on the team already uses. The goal is frictionless retrieval; anything that adds friction kills the library.

Store each SOP with two pieces of metadata: the **owner** (the person responsible for keeping it accurate) and the **last review date**. These two fields are what enable the quarterly review ritual below.

## The quarterly review ritual

Once a quarter, each SOP owner spends 15 minutes on their assigned SOPs. They read the SOP, walk through the steps mentally, and ask one question: "Does this still match how the work actually happens?"

If yes, they update the "last review" field to today's date and move on.

If no, they update the SOP with the current process. This is where AI saves time again — the owner opens Claude, pastes the existing SOP, says "here's what changed: [description]," and the AI produces an updated version in the same format. 5 minutes per outdated SOP instead of 30 minutes of manual rewriting.

Total quarterly cost: 15 minutes × 10 SOPs = 2.5 hours every three months for the whole agency. That is the single most reliable investment you can make in agency durability. An SOP library that is reviewed quarterly stays accurate for years. One that is written once and never touched is wrong within 6 months.

The quarterly review is the difference between the library being alive or dead. It is not optional. Put it on the calendar the same day you finish the initial ten SOPs.

## The SOP superpower

Once you have a real SOP library running with the quarterly review in place, you can do something most agencies cannot: onboard a new team member in days instead of weeks. You hand them the library link, assign them one SOP per morning, and have them shadow you on the same process in the afternoon. By the end of week one they are productive on four of the ten procedures. By end of week two, all ten. You have reduced the ramp time for a new CSR from six weeks of tribal-knowledge shadowing to two weeks of documented ramp-up.

That speed changes the hiring math. If you can productive-ramp a new CSR in two weeks, you can hire more easily, test more roles, and take on more capacity without the usual "we can't afford to train anyone right now" excuse. The SOP library is the thing that makes the agency an actual operation instead of a personal practice with employees.

## Do this today

Open Claude or ChatGPT. Paste the co-author prompt. Pick the one SOP that would most help someone replace you on your first day off — for most owners that is either the renewal outreach sequence or the new policy binding process. Start talking. 15 minutes later you will have a complete, structured SOP for the most-in-your-head process in the agency.

That one SOP is proof the approach works. It is also the one SOP that matters most to the durability of the agency. Everything else in the library is incrementally easier once the first one is done and you have felt how fast the drafting actually is.
