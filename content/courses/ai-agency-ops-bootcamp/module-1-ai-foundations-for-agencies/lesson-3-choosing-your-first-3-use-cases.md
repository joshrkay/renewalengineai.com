# Choosing Your First 3 Use Cases

You cannot roll out ten AI workflows at once. You can roll out three. This lesson is about picking the three that produce the biggest visible wins so that your team believes in the system before the novelty wears off.

I have watched agencies try to deploy eight AI workflows simultaneously. I have never seen one succeed. I have watched agencies deploy three workflows, see wins in two weeks, and then add the fourth and fifth three months later from a position of confidence. I have seen that succeed almost every time. The difference is not about capability. It is about focus, momentum, and internal buy-in.

## The selection criteria

A good first use case meets four criteria. If a use case fails any one of them, drop it from your short list and pick something else.

**High recurrence.** It happens at least five times per week. If the workflow triggers only twice a month, nobody remembers how to use it, the CSR forgets the exact step-by-step, and the tool gets abandoned within 30 days. Infrequent workflows are real — quarterly reports, annual audits, rare document types — but they are not first use cases. Save them for Module 5 when your team is fluent.

**Low stakes.** The cost of an imperfect output is small. No E&O exposure, no regulatory risk, no client relationship damage if a draft needs a bit of editing. You are not trying to prove AI is bulletproof in week one. You are trying to prove it saves time without hurting anyone. Start safe.

**Visible time savings.** The person doing the task today can immediately feel how much faster it got. This is what creates internal momentum. A workflow that saves 30 seconds here and 30 seconds there is real savings, but it is invisible, and invisible savings do not make anyone believe. A workflow that takes a 20-minute task down to 3 minutes makes believers.

**Single tool.** The first three use cases should each work with just your chat AI plus one other tool (usually email or your spreadsheet). Workflows that need three tools chained together, a webhook, and a custom schedule are Module 2 territory, not Module 1. Keep it simple. Prove the concept. Add complexity only after the simple version is running every day without drama.

## A menu of options

Here is a starter menu. Pick three. Resist the urge to pick five. The fifth one will sink the other four.

**Renewal outreach drafting.** (From the AI for Agent Retention course. If you took that, you already have this one.) High recurrence, low stakes, visible savings. The CSR pastes the client's policy details into the chat AI, gets a personalized draft, edits in 30 seconds, sends. 20 minutes of drafting becomes 2 minutes. Strong pick for any agency.

**New client welcome emails.** Every new policy bound triggers a warm welcome. AI drafts a personalized message referencing the specific policy, the coverage highlights, the next steps, and the CSR who bound it. CSR approves, send. Used to be 15 to 20 minutes of writing. Becomes 90 seconds. High recurrence in any growing book.

**COI request drafting.** Commercial clients need Certificates of Insurance constantly — every new vendor, every project, every contract renewal. AI reads the request (often a forwarded email from the client's procurement team), drafts the response with the policy details pulled from your AMS export, human approves, send. Previously a 10 to 15 minute task done 20 times a week. Becomes a 2-minute task. If you have a commercial-heavy book, this is the single highest ROI first use case.

**Claims follow-up drafting.** AI drafts the check-in messages for clients with active claims. "Hi Jennifer, just checking in on how the adjuster visit went yesterday. Let me know if you need anything this week." Human edits for empathy, specifics, and the particular tone the client needs, then sends. Especially valuable because check-in messages are the ones CSRs most often forget to send when the week gets busy. AI makes the drafting frictionless so the check-ins actually happen.

**Lead intake triage.** AI reads incoming lead inquiries (from your website form, from referral partners, from carrier leads) and classifies them by urgency (ready-to-quote vs. nurture), line of business (PL vs. CL), and fit (in your appetite vs. decline). The classification goes into a spreadsheet or into your CRM, and you wake up Monday morning with a prioritized list instead of an inbox you have to manually sort. Strong pick for lead-heavy agencies.

**Proposal language drafting.** AI writes the explanatory text in commercial proposals based on the carriers and coverages selected. Not the numbers — those come from the quoting tool. The narrative. "This proposal provides X coverage from Y carrier, chosen for these specific reasons given your exposure profile..." Saves 30 minutes per proposal on a task producers notoriously procrastinate.

**Cross-sell suggestion drafting.** AI looks at a client's current coverage and suggests which cross-sell opportunity fits them best (auto-to-home, home-to-umbrella, PL-to-CL for small business owners), with a drafted outreach message. You do not send the draft as-is — you use it as a starting point for a personal follow-up. This one is especially good for personal lines producers who know they should cross-sell more but never make the time.

## The three-week test

![The three-week use-case test: Week 1 setup, Week 2 live pilot, Week 3 decide — ship, kill, or scale](/courses/bootcamp/three-week-test.svg)

Once you pick three, give each one three weeks of runway. Week one is setup, prompt tuning, and training. Weeks two and three are running the workflow for real with the actual person who will own it.

At the end of week three, measure three things:

1. **Did it save time?** Benchmark the pre-AI time and the post-AI time on five instances. Real numbers, not guesses.
2. **Did the quality hold up?** Review the last 20 outputs. Count the ones that required "substantial editing" versus "quick approval." Aim for 80 percent quick approvals.
3. **Did the team actually use it?** Count the number of times the workflow was used in weeks two and three. If the count is lower than the volume of the task should have produced, the workflow is not being used — the old manual way is still happening in parallel. That is a red flag.

Any workflow that fails the three-week test gets dropped, not fixed. Cutting losses fast is more important than protecting sunk cost. You can always come back to a failed workflow later with more information, a better prompt, or a different CSR championing it.

## Why three and not one

One workflow is fragile. If the single workflow runs into a problem in week two, the whole "we're using AI now" narrative dies. The team concludes AI does not work, and you cannot re-start the conversation for six months. Three workflows create resilience. One can stumble and two can still prove the point.

More than three dilutes focus. Five workflows means none of them are your top priority, which means none of them really land, which means your team gets the worst of both worlds — a bunch of half-working tools and no clear win to rally around.

Three is the number. Not two, not five. Three.

## Who owns each workflow

One more design decision. Each of your three workflows needs a named human owner who is responsible for running it, tuning the prompt, and flagging when it breaks. Do not make "the agency" the owner. Make a specific person the owner.

If you have a CSR team, assign each workflow to one CSR. If you are solo, you are the owner of all three — but name yourself explicitly and put the ownership on your weekly review list. Ownerless workflows die fast.

## Common mistakes

**Picking workflows that are too rare.** "We get one of these requests a week" is not high recurrence. It is low recurrence dressed up.

**Picking workflows that sound cool but save no visible time.** "AI-assisted carrier appetite research" sounds great and saves seven minutes per quote. Seven minutes is not visible. Skip it for now.

**Picking workflows your team has political resistance to.** If the CSR handling COI requests feels threatened by AI taking over her favorite task, that workflow will not succeed even if it technically works. Pick something else. Come back when the culture shifts.

**Picking workflows that require data you don't have clean.** If the workflow needs every client to have a current phone number and you are at 60 percent coverage, the workflow will fail because of the data, not the AI. Fix the data first.

## Do this today

Look at the menu above. Circle the three that match your agency's specific volume and pain points. Write them on a sticky note and put it on your monitor. Those are your next three weeks.

Name an owner for each one. If it is you, write your name. If it is a CSR, walk over and ask them today whether they want to own it — do not assume.

You now have three use cases, three owners, and a three-week measurement plan. That is Module 1. You are ready for the actual building.
