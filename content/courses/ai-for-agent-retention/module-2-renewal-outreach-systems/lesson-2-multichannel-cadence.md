# Multichannel Cadence — Email, SMS, Voice

A sequence that lives only in email is leaving response rate on the table. Every independent agent has clients who never open email but answer texts within minutes, and clients who will pick up a call but delete everything else. This lesson shows you how to layer channels without feeling intrusive, which channel to use for which touch, and the guardrails that keep you out of TCPA trouble.

By the end of this lesson you will have a channel rulebook, a practical layered cadence for each of your three client tiers, and a gut-check test that catches the moment a sequence stops feeling like service and starts feeling like marketing.

## The three channels and what they are for

Think about each channel as having a specific tolerance for intrusion and a specific job. If you use a channel for a job it was not built for, you create friction. Every case of "this AI retention thing is annoying my clients" I have diagnosed traces back to using the wrong channel for the wrong touch.

**Email** is low-intrusion, low-urgency, and high-content. Use it for the long touches: the value reminder, the review offer, the formal renewal delivery, the thank-you note. Email is where you deliver anything that needs to be read later, forwarded, searched, or saved. Email tolerates length. Clients do not resent a 300-word email from their agent the way they resent a 300-word text message. Email is also the channel with the lowest compliance risk — you are not going to get sued for sending a polite follow-up email.

The downside of email is that open rates on renewal-related mail sit between 25 and 45 percent for most agencies. Which means more than half your clients never saw the message you spent time on. That is why we layer.

**SMS** is high-intrusion, medium-urgency, and strictly low-content. Use it sparingly and for three purposes only: a short "just making sure you saw this" nudge 24 hours after an important email goes unopened; the X-7 renewal reminder; and quick service-related follow-ups ("adjuster is calling you tomorrow at 10, please confirm"). Never cold-sell over SMS. Never send a long message. Never send marketing content. Three sentences maximum, always signed by a real name, always with a clear reason for the text. If you can't explain in one sentence why you're texting, don't.

The upside of SMS is that response rates sit between 45 and 75 percent on a healthy book. The downside is that every text is a small interruption of a real person's day, and if you abuse that, you are off their phone forever and there is no path back.

**Voice** (a phone call, or a voice note through a tool like WhatsApp Business or iMessage) is the highest-intrusion channel and the highest response channel. Reserve it for your top-quintile accounts, save conversations, and any situation where a written message would be misread as cold. The goal of a voice call in a retention context is almost never to deliver information. It is to restore the relationship. Voice is the channel you use when the conversation itself is the value.

The economics of voice are brutal. A 15-minute call with the phone number lookup, the voicemail, the callback, and the notes afterward is really 25 minutes of your time. A solo agent has capacity for maybe 8 retention calls per week. Protect that budget ruthlessly.

## A practical layered cadence by tier

The right cadence depends on where the client sits in your book. One cadence for everyone is always wrong — either over-serving the bottom (which burns hours) or under-serving the top (which loses revenue).

**Top 20 percent of the book (by premium).** All seven touches from Lesson 1, with the X-14 touch as a live phone call and a personal sender on every email. Budget: roughly 40 minutes of human time per renewal across all touches, most of that on the call. These clients justify every minute.

**Middle 60 percent.** Six touches: X-90 email, X-60 email, X-45 email, X-30 email, X-14 check-in email with SMS fallback if unopened after 48 hours, X+1 thank you. Drop the X-7 SMS and the phone call. Budget: about 4 minutes of human time per renewal across all touches (mostly approval clicks). These clients get AI-drafted messages end to end.

**Bottom 20 percent.** Three touches: X-60 email, X-30 email, X+1 thank you. That is enough to keep the relationship warm without spending any real time on accounts that will not change the P&L either way. Budget: under 2 minutes of human time per renewal. If one of these clients surfaces a service issue, they immediately get upgraded to the middle tier's cadence for the rest of the cycle.

Match the effort to the revenue. Everything else is wishful thinking.

## The second-channel fallback rule

Here is the most valuable trick in multichannel: if a priority email is not opened within 48 hours, fire a single SMS that references the email. One text, once. "Hey Jane, just wanted to make sure the note I sent Tuesday about your renewal landed okay. Let me know if anything is unclear." That single fallback text converts the unopened email into a 60 to 70 percent reply rate on most books.

Do not automate this fallback blindly. Restrict it to the top two tiers, and restrict it to the touches that actually matter (the value recap and the rate preview). An SMS fallback on every email is spam. An SMS fallback on the two touches that drive retention is service.

## Avoid the "I'm being hunted" feeling

The single biggest mistake new agents make is firing all of these touches from a marketing automation tool that makes them read like a marketing sequence. Branded header images, unsubscribe footers, tracking pixels, generic salutations. The client opens the email, feels the template, and closes it.

Run every message through this gut check: *"If I got this from my dentist, would I think they cared about me or that I was in a funnel?"* If it's the second one, rewrite it. The fix is usually to strip the branding, change the sender to a real person, and remove the marketing boilerplate.

The other gut check: *would I be embarrassed if this client forwarded this to a friend with the comment "look how cold this is"?* If yes, rewrite. If the client would forward it with "look how thoughtful my agent is," ship it.

## A note on SMS opt-in and TCPA

Every SMS touch requires documented opt-in. Do not cut corners here. The trial lawyer industry finds TCPA violations in five minutes on LinkedIn, and a single class-action settlement dwarfs any retention gain you'd ever make from texting without consent.

Practical opt-in collection: add a "text me for quick updates" checkbox on your quote forms, your website contact form, and your client onboarding paperwork. Log the opt-in in your AMS with a date. When you query the book for your SMS fallback list, filter by the opt-in flag. No flag, no text. Easy.

If you run an older book that never collected SMS consent, don't try to backfill it with a mass "click yes to opt in" email — that is itself compliance-adjacent. Instead, add the opt-in question to your next round of renewal phone calls and review emails. Collect consent organically as clients come through the cycle. Within 18 months your whole book is opted in.

## What the cadence looks like end to end

Tie it all together: on any given Monday, a middle-tier client who is 46 days from renewal has just received their value recap email. An AI-drafted version landed in your approval queue that morning, you read it in 10 seconds, clicked approve, and it went out. The client probably hasn't replied. That is fine — the goal of the X-45 touch is not a reply, it is to plant the thought.

Two weeks later that same client gets the rate preview email. If they don't open it within 48 hours, they get a one-sentence SMS from you. They reply to the text with a question about the rate increase. You jump on the reply, answer it personally, and the renewal is locked a week before it delivers.

That entire sequence consumed about six minutes of your time across three weeks, delivered three touches on two channels, and produced a retained client. Without the system, this client probably gets one renewal packet email and 50-50 odds of shopping around. That is the ROI of a multichannel cadence in one client's lifecycle.

## Do this today

Open your phone. Pick the last three clients whose policies renewed in the past month. Send each of them a personal SMS: *"Hey {first_name}, how did everything go with the renewal? Any questions I can answer?"*

Count how many reply within two hours. Most agents are surprised by the number. That is the unused power of the second channel, sitting in your pocket right now.
