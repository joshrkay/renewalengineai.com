# Segmenting At-Risk Clients for Outreach

Once you have your weekly at-risk list from the dashboard, the next question is what to do with it. Every at-risk client is not the same. Some need a 15-minute phone call from you personally. Some need a thoughtful AI-drafted email you approve and send. Some need nothing more than the standard trigger sequence to keep running. Treating all three the same way is how retention systems burn out — either by over-working the calls list or by under-investing in the accounts that actually drive revenue.

This lesson gives you the three-bucket sorting system that works, the rule that keeps you sane, and the specific workflow for each bucket. By the end you will be able to take a 20-person at-risk list and know within 90 seconds exactly who gets what.

## The three-bucket system

At-risk clients fall cleanly into three buckets. Your job is to spend 30 seconds per client sorting them, then follow the template for each bucket. This is a sorting problem, not a judgment problem. Resist the temptation to treat every at-risk client as a unique case — you don't have the time, and the sorting rules are reliable enough that 90 percent of the time the bucket is obvious.

**Bucket 1 — Save candidates.** Top 20 percent of the book (by premium or lifetime value), with at least one hard churn signal (significant rate increase, payment friction, known life event, or long silence). These are the clients who will walk if you don't pick up the phone. You owe these clients a live voice conversation before the renewal delivers. No email substitute. No "I'll get to it next week." Call, or lose them.

Typical week: 3 to 5 clients fall into this bucket on a 600 to 800 policy book.

**Bucket 2 — Reassurance candidates.** Middle-tier accounts (middle 60 percent of the book) with one risk signal but a long tenure, a good relationship history, and no active downgrade or payment drama. These clients usually stay if they hear from you at all. They do not need a phone call — that would actually be strange and feel like a sales move. They need a thoughtful, personalized email from your actual account that references their specific situation and invites a reply.

Typical week: 8 to 15 clients.

**Bucket 3 — Automated candidates.** Bottom-tier accounts with low risk signals, or any client where the standard trigger sequence is already doing its job. Run the sequence, do not invent a custom touch, and move on. If they lapse, the revenue cost is small and you have bigger fish to fry. The entire point of Bucket 3 is to protect your attention from being fragmented across accounts that do not change the P&L.

Typical week: everyone else on the at-risk list, usually 5 to 10 clients.

## The rule that keeps you sane

You cannot call everyone. A solo agent running a 600-policy book realistically has capacity for 5 to 7 save conversations per week. Maybe 10 if the week is light. Protect that budget ruthlessly, or the whole system collapses into "I did retention this week" without the retention actually happening.

Spending your save-call budget on bottom-bucket accounts is the classic failure mode. The agent feels productive because they made calls — calls feel like real work — but the book still bleeds because the top accounts never got a call. This failure is psychological, not analytical. Calling a warm, easy, small-premium client *feels* better than calling a difficult, awkward, high-premium client about a rate increase. The easy calls are the wrong calls.

If you catch yourself reaching for a Bucket 3 client on Wednesday afternoon because the Bucket 1 call feels hard, stop. The hard call is the one that matters. Make the hard call or do literally anything else. Do not make the easy call as a substitute.

## The 90-second sorting workflow

Once a week, right after the dashboard ritual from Lesson 3.2, take your at-risk list and sort it into the three buckets. Add three columns: Bucket, Action, Deadline.

- **Bucket** is 1, 2, or 3.
- **Action** is the specific verb: "Call," "Draft email," "Let sequence run."
- **Deadline** is the day by which the action must happen. Never "this week." Always a specific day.

Sort the list by bucket. Do Bucket 1 first. Block the call times on your calendar immediately, before you move to Bucket 2. If the calls are not on the calendar before you close the sheet, they will not happen.

Bucket 2 goes next. Open your AI-drafting workflow from Lesson 2.3 and generate the drafts for every Bucket 2 client in one sitting. Approve and send them in a batch. Budget 20 minutes for the whole batch. This is the most leveraged 20 minutes of your retention work.

Bucket 3 takes zero effort. You confirm the sequence is running, you update the row to "monitoring," and you move on.

## Bucket 1 — the anatomy of a save call

A save call is not a sales call. The failure mode of new agents is to treat it like one, pitching the client on why they should stay. That never works on a client who is already half out the door. The play is different.

**Open with gratitude, not urgency.** "Hey {first_name}, {agent_name} calling. I was reviewing your account this morning and wanted to check in directly. How are things?" That is the entire opening. Do not mention the renewal in the first 30 seconds. The client knows why you're calling. Your job is to make the call feel like a relationship check, not a sales pull.

**Listen for the real concern.** Most at-risk clients, once you open the conversation, will tell you what's bothering them within the first three minutes — a rate increase they're upset about, a claim that went poorly, a life event you didn't know about. Your job is to listen, not to solve. Let them say the whole thing before you respond to any piece of it.

**Address the concern with one specific action.** "I hear you. Let me look into two things this afternoon and come back to you by Thursday with options." Specific, short, concrete. Do not make vague promises. Do not over-commit. Do not offer a discount you don't have the authority to give.

**Follow through within the time you promised.** More save calls fail on the follow-through than on the call itself. If you said Thursday, Thursday means Thursday. If Thursday becomes impossible, text them Wednesday night and reschedule. Silence after a save call is the worst possible outcome.

A good save call plus a good follow-through saves roughly 70 percent of Bucket 1 accounts on a healthy book. Without the call, the same accounts lapse at roughly 60 percent. That swing — 70 percent saved versus 60 percent lapsed — is the single biggest retention lever in the whole course.

## Bucket 2 — the AI-drafted reassurance email

Bucket 2 is the sleeper bucket. It's where AI-assisted outreach earns its keep. Ten reassurance emails drafted in five minutes by an AI, personalized with real policy facts, approved and sent in ten minutes. That is a 15-minute investment that retains three to five policies per week on a typical book.

The prompt is a small variation on the personalization prompt from Lesson 2.3. Add one instruction: *"This client has been flagged as at-risk because {specific signal from the dashboard}. Acknowledge the signal obliquely without being alarming, and invite the client to reply with any concerns."* That one sentence turns a standard personalized touch into a reassurance touch without sounding like a panic move.

Over a year, Bucket 2 alone retains 150 to 250 policies on a typical book. At a couple hundred dollars of commission per policy, the math speaks for itself. You will never notice Bucket 2 working because the clients never would have announced they were thinking about leaving — they just don't leave. That is exactly what a healthy book looks like.

## Don't skip the easy wins

The single biggest tactical mistake new agents make running this system is to under-invest in Bucket 2 because the clients don't *feel* at risk. They're middle-tier accounts who pay on time and don't complain. Why spend 15 minutes on them?

Because 60 percent of your lapse cost comes from the middle tier, not the top. The top tier makes noise when they're leaving. The middle tier leaves silently. Bucket 2 is how you catch the silent churners before they drift off your book. Skipping Bucket 2 because the clients seem fine is exactly how agencies lose them.

## A note on over-triage

Do not spend 20 minutes sorting a 15-person at-risk list. The sorting should take 90 seconds, not 20 minutes. If you find yourself over-analyzing which bucket a client belongs in, default to Bucket 2. Bucket 2 is the safest miss — a reassurance email to a client who was never at risk costs nothing and strengthens the relationship; a save call to a client who was never at risk wastes 25 minutes of your week. When in doubt, Bucket 2.

## Do this today

Take your top 10 upcoming renewals from last week's dashboard. Assign each one to Bucket 1, 2, or 3. See how many are Bucket 1. That is your save list for this week. Block the call times on your calendar before you close this tab.

If all 10 came out as Bucket 3, your sorting criteria are too loose and you are not looking hard enough at the signals. Go back to the five signals from Lesson 3.1 and score harder.
