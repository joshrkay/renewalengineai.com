# Building a Book Health Dashboard

A dashboard for a solo or small agency does not mean Tableau, Power BI, or a $500-a-month analytics platform. It means one page you look at on Monday morning that tells you where to spend your time this week. If the dashboard takes more than 30 seconds to read, it is too complex and you will stop using it. If it has more than five metrics, it is too complex and you will stop using it. This lesson shows you how to build a working book-health dashboard in under an hour and, more importantly, how to actually use it every week.

By the end of this lesson you will have a Google Sheet with the five metrics that matter, a ritual for refreshing it that fits into your Monday morning, and a clear rule for when you are allowed to look at anything else.

## The only five metrics that belong on it

Resist the temptation to track twenty KPIs. A working book-health dashboard has five numbers. Not four, not seven. Five. Anything else is decoration that you will delete after two months when you realize you never look at it.

**Metric 1 — Renewals in the next 30 days (count and total premium).** This is your immediate workload. It tells you how busy the next four weeks are going to be and whether you need to clear space on the calendar. On a typical 800-policy book this hovers around 65 to 70 renewals per month; a week when it spikes to 90 is a week to cancel anything non-essential.

**Metric 2 — Renewals with premium increase over your threshold (count).** Use the thresholds from Lesson 3.1: 12 percent on personal auto, 15 percent on home, 8 percent on commercial GL. This is your at-risk workload — the renewals that need proactive rate-preview conversations before they deliver. If this number is above 15 percent of Metric 1, you have a structural rate problem coming and you need to start those conversations now.

**Metric 3 — Untouched top-20 accounts (count).** The number of top-20-percent clients who have not received any outreach from you or your team in the last 60 days. This is your *relationship debt*. If it is above zero, you have clients who think you have forgotten them. Debt compounds. Pay it down every week.

**Metric 4 — This week's save list (count, 3 to 7).** The specific accounts you personally need to call this week. Not automate. Not email. Call. This number should always be between three and seven — fewer than three means you are not looking hard enough at the at-risk list, more than seven means you are about to not call most of them.

**Metric 5 — Lapse count, month to date.** One number. Unemotional. This is how you know if the system is working. Compare it to the same month last year. If it's flat, keep going. If it's down, celebrate quietly. If it's up, do not panic — look at the cause before you change the system. One bad month does not invalidate a working year.

That is the entire dashboard. Five cells. If a sixth metric ever seems important, find a cell to delete first. The discipline is not which metrics to add. It is which metrics to refuse.

## The cheapest version (and it's enough)

Open Google Sheets. Create a new sheet called "book-health." Add six columns and five rows:

| Metric | This Week | Last Week | 4 Wks Ago | Trend | Action |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Renewals next 30 days | | | | | |
| Renewals w/ rate shock | | | | | |
| Untouched top-20 | | | | | |
| Save list (count) | | | | | |
| Lapses MTD | | | | | |

That is the dashboard. Total build time: six minutes. Refresh it every Monday morning by pulling the numbers from your AMS and pasting them in. The "trend" column is a single arrow (↑ ↓ →) you type manually. The "action" column is a note to yourself about what you will do this week based on the numbers.

Most agents running this system successfully never get past the Google Sheet version. Fancy tooling is not what makes it work. The ritual is what makes it work.

## The medium version (only if you want it)

If you want the dashboard slightly fancier, use Airtable or Notion. Both let you link the save-list cell directly to a table of at-risk clients with each one's name, last contact date, next planned touch, and notes. The clickable rows turn the dashboard into a workspace instead of a summary.

Airtable is better for this if you already use it. Notion is better if you already use Notion. Don't adopt a new tool for the dashboard — that is how the whole thing dies in week three. Use whatever you already know.

## The automated version (optional, not essential)

The highest-effort version connects your AMS export to a Google Apps Script or Zapier that writes the values into the sheet automatically once a week. Setup time is three to four hours the first time. Payoff is about eight minutes per week saved on the manual refresh.

That math only works out if you are going to run the ritual for a full year. Most agents are better off staying on the manual version for the first 90 days, proving they will actually do the Monday ritual, and only investing in automation once they have earned the confidence that the ritual is permanent. Automating a ritual you don't actually do is worse than not automating it — it creates the illusion of a system where there isn't one.

## The Monday ritual is the actual system

The dashboard is worthless without the 30-minute Monday ritual. The ritual is:

1. Open the dashboard.
2. Pull the five numbers (five minutes).
3. Update the trend arrows (one minute).
4. Look at the save list and pick the three to seven clients you will personally call this week (ten minutes).
5. Block call time on your calendar this week, right now, before you leave the sheet (five minutes).
6. Write one sentence in the "action" column about what you are going to change this week based on what you see (two minutes).
7. Close the sheet and do not look at it again until next Monday (optional but highly recommended).

That is the system. The dashboard is the tool. The ritual is the system. If the dashboard is perfect but the ritual does not happen, the dashboard is decoration.

## A few rules about the ritual

Three rules learned the hard way from agencies that have run this for a year.

**Same time every week.** Pick a window — usually 8:30 to 9:00 am Monday — and protect it. If you do the ritual at random times, it will drift into not happening at all within six weeks.

**Do it alone.** No meetings, no email, no phone. The 30 minutes is a thinking ritual, not a team sync. You can share the outputs with your team afterward, but the dashboard review happens in isolation.

**Never skip. Never apologize. Never catch up.** If you skip a Monday, you do not try to catch up on Tuesday. You wait until next Monday and you do the ritual as normal. Skipped weeks happen. They do not break the system. Trying to "catch up" breaks the system by turning it into a guilt object.

## What the numbers tell you over time

After three months of weekly ritual, patterns start to emerge. Common ones:

- Lapse count MTD starts flat and begins to drop around month 3 as the trigger system gets into full swing.
- Untouched-top-20 starts high (often 8 to 15) and drops to zero or one within six weeks once you start actually working the dashboard.
- Save list volume grows for the first month (you're finding issues you missed before) and then stabilizes at 4 to 6 clients per week.
- Renewals with rate shock spikes during the carrier's annual rate actions and drops between actions — predictable once you see the pattern.

None of these are surprising, but seeing them in your own book for the first time is surprisingly motivating. The dashboard turns retention from a vague worry into a measurable, moving number you are personally responsible for. That shift — from worry to ownership — is what separates agencies that run this system from agencies that try it for a month and quit.

## Do this today

Create the Google Sheet with the five rows listed above. Leave the values blank for now. Share the link with yourself in a calendar invite for next Monday 8:30 am titled "Book health ritual — 30 min."

Next Monday you will fill in the numbers for the first time. The Monday after that will be the second. By the fourth Monday, the ritual will be a habit. By the twelfth Monday, it will be the most valuable 30 minutes of your week.
