# Measuring ROI and Iterating

A system you cannot measure is a system you cannot defend. And a system you cannot defend will be the first thing you cut when a big client complains, when a new hire needs training, or when you have a bad week and start second-guessing everything. The Monday ritual will not survive its first real challenge unless you can point at a number and say "this is working, leave it alone."

This lesson walks through the three numbers that prove your retention work is paying off, the monthly review routine that keeps them honest, the quarterly iteration cadence that improves the system without over-tweaking it, and the defensive framing you use when someone asks why you are spending three hours a week on something that looks like nothing from the outside.

By the end of this lesson you will know exactly which numbers to track, the one iteration rule that prevents over-engineering, and the one-paragraph defense you can recite to yourself at 10pm on a Tuesday when the ritual feels like it's not worth it.

## Metric 1 — Retention rate

The headline metric. The percentage of policies that renewed in a given window. Calculate it monthly. The formula is:

> Retention rate = (policies with X-date in month − policies that lapsed) ÷ policies with X-date in month × 100

Pull the numerator from your AMS. Pull the denominator from the same renewal list you used for last month's dashboard. Compare this month's number to the same month *last year*, not to last month. Month-over-month is noisy because different months have different renewal mixes; year-over-year controls for that.

What to do with the number:

- **Flat (within 1 percentage point).** The system is not yet working, or not yet long enough. Do not change the system. Check the quality of your drafts (are they actually going out, or stuck in approval?) and the consistency of the Monday ritual. Most "flat" months are actually "ritual-slipped" months.
- **Up 2 or more points.** You have a working system. Celebrate quietly and keep going. Do not add new features. Do not try to double the improvement. A 2-point lift sustained is worth more than a 4-point lift that breaks in month three.
- **Down 2 or more points.** One month is statistical noise on a 65-policy-per-month renewal window. Wait for the second month before drawing any conclusion. If two consecutive months are down, *then* re-read Module 3 and look for a systematic miss.

## Metric 2 — Recovered revenue

This is the number that makes the case to your business partner, your spouse, or yourself at 10pm when you are tempted to stop doing the Monday ritual. Retention rate is the percentage. Recovered revenue is the dollars.

The formula:

> Recovered revenue = (this month's retention rate − baseline retention rate from Module 1) × this month's total renewal premium

Worked example. Your baseline retention from Module 1 was 84 percent. This month it is 86 percent. Your total renewal premium for the month is $62,000. The recovered revenue is (0.86 − 0.84) × $62,000 = **$1,240** for the month. Annualized on a consistent 2-point lift, that is roughly **$14,880 per year** of renewal premium that stayed on the book because of the system.

Apply your commission rate to turn that into agency income. At a 12 percent average commission, that's roughly $1,800 of commission saved this year from a system costing under $200 a year in tools. That is a 9-to-1 return in the first year, before any compounding effect on lifetime value or referrals.

Recovered revenue is also the single best number for defending the work. Nobody argues with a dollar figure attached to a consistent month-over-month trend. "The retention system saved us $14,880 last year" is a sentence that ends any internal debate about whether the three hours a week are worth it.

## Metric 3 — Hours spent

Track how much time you personally spend on retention activities each week. Not your total work hours. Just the ritual: drafting outreach, running the dashboard, approving AI drafts, making save calls, doing the Bucket 1 follow-through. This should be between **3 and 6 hours per week** on a typical book once the system is running past day 90.

If it is over 6 hours a week, something is inefficient. Check:

- Are you drafting messages from scratch instead of using the AI prompt? That alone can consume 4+ hours.
- Are you calling Bucket 2 accounts that should get emailed? Save calls are expensive in time and the bucket rules exist to protect you.
- Are you re-doing the Monday ritual from scratch each week instead of reusing the sheet?

If it is under 3 hours a week, you are almost certainly cutting corners. The most common version of this is skipping the save calls entirely, which shows up in the save-rate sub-metric (below) before it shows up in retention rate.

## The monthly review routine

On the last Monday of each month, take an extra 20 minutes after the normal ritual and do the following:

1. Calculate retention rate for the month just ending. Write the number in a running log (one cell per month).
2. Calculate recovered revenue using the baseline. Write it next to the retention rate.
3. Note hours spent on retention work for the month (sum your four weekly rituals).
4. Compute save rate: of the Bucket 1 clients you called this month, how many retained? Healthy is above 50 percent; great is above 70 percent.
5. Write one sentence answering: "What should I change next month?" Usually the honest answer is "nothing."

That running log is the single most valuable artifact the system produces. It is also the thing you show anyone who asks what you're doing differently.

## Quarterly iteration cadence

Once a quarter, sit down for a full hour with the running log and ask three questions. Not more. Not weekly.

**Question 1 — What actually changed?** A retention rate that moved needs an explanation. Did you change the cadence? Did you add a channel? Did the market shift carriers? Write down the cause you believe is responsible. If you cannot identify a cause, the movement is probably noise and you should not react to it.

**Question 2 — Where did the saves come from?** Go back to your at-risk lists from the last 13 weeks and check which flagged accounts retained. That is your save rate. Above 70 percent, the system is very strong. Between 50 and 70, it is healthy. Below 50, either your sorting is too aggressive (flagging clients who were never going to lapse) or your messaging is not landing. Check the drafts before blaming the signals.

**Question 3 — What is the next bottleneck?** Every system has one. Is it the time you spend on drafts (fix: tighten the prompt or update voice samples)? The quality of the Bucket 1 calls (fix: script the opening)? The volume of untouched top-20 accounts (fix: add a standing X-180 touch)? Pick *one* bottleneck per quarter and improve it. Do not try to fix three things simultaneously — you will lose track of which change caused which result.

The discipline is the same as Module 3's dashboard discipline: pick the single highest-leverage change, run it for a full quarter, then measure before touching anything else.

## The most common iteration

Nine out of ten agents find the exact same bottleneck in their first quarterly review: **they are not actually calling the save-candidate list consistently.** The weekly ritual catches the list but the calls slip off the calendar. The Monday sort identifies four Bucket 1 clients; by Friday only one got called; the other three lapse quietly over the next 60 days.

The fix is not a new tool. It is not a better AI prompt. It is not more training. The fix is **protecting the call block on the calendar and treating it like a client appointment.** If you block Tuesday 2-4pm on Monday morning for "Save calls — Bucket 1," and you honor it the same way you would honor a client meeting, the retention rate moves without any other change. Nine out of ten first-quarter improvements come from exactly this one discipline.

The second most common bottleneck is stale voice samples. You wrote them in month one, your tone has evolved, and the drafts now sound like last year's you. Fix is 20 minutes of sent-folder harvesting. Done.

## The 10pm defense

Here is the paragraph to recite to yourself, your spouse, or your business partner when the ritual feels like it's not worth it:

> "I spend three hours a week on retention work. My baseline lapse rate was X percent on a $Y book. The system has lifted retention by Z points, which is $W of recovered premium a month and roughly $AA of recovered commission a year. The tools cost $BB a month. The ROI is CC to 1. I am not guessing — I have a running log and I know exactly what the system is producing."

Fill in the blanks from your running log. Memorize it. The first time a business partner or a family member questions the time, you will deliver it in one breath and the conversation will end. Any system you cannot defend in one paragraph will get cut the first time it is inconvenient. This paragraph is the insurance policy on the insurance retention system.

## What not to iterate on

A short list of things that look like bottlenecks but are not:

- **Tool switching.** If the system is producing real numbers, the tool is fine. Switching from Zapier to Make.com in month four kills three weeks of momentum and changes nothing in the metrics.
- **Adding channels.** If email and SMS are working, do not add direct mail or cold voicemail in month four. The complexity cost is higher than the marginal lift.
- **Re-segmenting the book.** The top-20 / middle-60 / bottom-20 split is robust. Do not re-draw it every quarter looking for a better cut.
- **Rewriting the prompt from scratch.** Tune existing prompts with small edits based on what you actually change in drafts. Do not throw out a prompt that produces 80 percent usable drafts to start over.

The system gets better by running longer, not by getting rebuilt.

## Do this today

Calculate your retention rate for the current month-to-date and write it next to your baseline from Module 1. That single gap is the number that matters. Then open a fresh sheet, label it "retention log," and add the first monthly row. You will thank yourself at the end of Q1 when you can point at four rows of data instead of one vague memory.
