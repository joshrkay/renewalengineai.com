# Your 90-Day Rollout Plan

Everything in the first three modules is easy to understand and hard to implement while also running a full agency. The gap between "I get it" and "it's running every week in my book" is where most retention projects die. This lesson is a 90-day plan that respects reality. It assumes you have a full inbox, an existing client base, and exactly zero free afternoons.

By the end of this lesson you will have a phase-by-phase plan, a realistic time budget for each week, a list of what to skip (just as important as what to do), and the day-91 measurement that tells you whether it worked.

![The 90-day retention rollout: three phases of Foundation, Launch, and Scale, each mapping to a 30-day window, with a measurement checkpoint at day 91](/courses/retention/90-day-rollout.svg)

## The operating principle

Before the plan itself, one rule: during these 90 days, you will not buy new tools, migrate AMS platforms, hire anyone, or take on any other large initiative. The rollout has to compete with the day job. If you add a second large project, neither finishes. Protect these 90 days the same way you would protect a product launch.

The total time commitment is roughly 2 to 4 hours per week on top of normal work, with a 3-hour front-loaded block in week one. Any agency that can find 3 hours a week can run this plan. The constraint is almost never time. It is discipline about *which* 3 hours.

## Phase 1 — Foundation (Days 1 to 30)

The first 30 days are about building the base so the touches have something to stand on. You are not sending anything to clients yet. Nothing in Phase 1 is client-facing. That is intentional — it removes the emotional pressure of "will this embarrass me" and lets you focus on mechanics.

**Week 1 — the 3-hour setup block.** One block, one sitting. During this block:
- Export your active policies from the AMS into a spreadsheet.
- Compute your real lapse cost using the formula from Lesson 1.2.
- Set a retention budget ceiling at 20 percent of that cost.
- Sign up for one chat AI (Claude, ChatGPT, or Gemini) on a business account and save your voice samples file (three real emails from your sent folder).
- Sign up for one workflow tool (Zapier or Make.com) on the cheapest tier.

That is the entire setup block. Write down "Retention system Week 1 block" on your calendar right now. If it is not on the calendar, it will not happen.

**Week 2 — book health sheet.** Create the Google Sheet dashboard from Lesson 3.2. Five rows, six columns, completely blank for now. Run your first Monday ritual on whatever data you have access to. Expect it to feel awkward. The ritual becomes a habit around week 6, not week 2.

**Week 3 — top-20 percent segmentation.** Sort your book by premium or lifetime value. Identify the top 20 percent. Export that list as a separate file. This is your priority segment for every touch system you build. Everything else in the book is served by standard automation; this slice is served by standard automation plus your personal attention.

**Week 4 — voice-mimic prompt draft.** Using the template from Lesson 2.3, write your reusable personalization prompt with your three voice samples pasted in. Save it somewhere you can reach it in two clicks. Test it by generating one draft manually for one real client in your head. Read the draft. If it sounds like you, you are done. If not, swap a voice sample and try again.

End of Phase 1. Nothing has gone out yet. You have: a real lapse cost, a budget ceiling, a book-health sheet, a top-20 segment, a voice-mimic prompt, and a subscription to two tools. Total cost so far: under $100. Total time spent: about 10 hours across four weeks.

## Phase 2 — Launch (Days 31 to 60)

The second 30 days are where the system starts touching real clients. This is where the emotional resistance kicks in — "what if the AI-drafted email sounds weird" — and the discipline is to ship anyway.

**Week 5 — wire the X-90 trigger.** In Zapier or Make, build exactly one automation: when a policy's X-date is 90 days out, pull the client facts and generate a draft using your prompt, and post the draft to a "pending approval" spreadsheet or Gmail draft folder. Nothing sends automatically. You approve every draft by hand.

**Week 6 — send the first wave.** Approve the X-90 drafts for 10 real clients in your top-20 segment. Read each draft carefully before approving. Edit where needed. Send. Notice what you edited. Those edits tell you what to tune in the prompt for week 7.

**Week 7 — wire X-60 and X-45 and start tuning.** Add two more triggers. Re-use the same prompt with different "occasion" text. Start your second wave of approved sends. Tune the prompt based on week 6 feedback.

**Week 8 — expand from top-20 to full book (middle tier).** Remove the top-20 filter on your triggers. Start drafting touches for the middle 60 percent of the book, with a slightly looser review standard (the 8-second Tier B from Lesson 2.3). Your weekly approval queue should now be around 30 to 60 drafts. Budget 20 minutes for the approval batch.

End of Phase 2. Emails are going out to real clients. The Monday ritual is becoming a habit. You are roughly 6 weeks into the program and starting to see the first response replies from clients. Most agents notice an uptick in warmth around this week — clients replying to say thanks, asking small questions, mentioning things they'd been meaning to bring up. That is the system starting to produce returns.

## Phase 3 — Scale (Days 61 to 90)

The third 30 days turn the system from a pilot into a durable routine.

**Week 9 — add SMS touches.** Wire the X-7 SMS touch for your top-20 segment. Confirm opt-in compliance first. Start sending. The SMS channel will produce the most visible reply spike of the whole rollout.

**Week 10 — launch lapse-risk scoring.** Start running the Monday at-risk prompt from Lesson 3.1. Feed it the five data exports. Get your first weekly ranked at-risk list. Run the three-bucket sort from Lesson 3.3. Make your first deliberate Bucket 1 save calls.

**Week 11 — weekly save-rate review.** At the end of the week, look at the Bucket 1 clients from week 10. How many retained? Note the save rate. This is the number you will watch for the rest of the program — more than retention rate, more than drafts sent, more than any other metric.

**Week 12 — document the SOP.** Before you forget how you built it, write a one-page SOP describing the weekly ritual, the prompt template, the trigger dates, and the sort rules. This document is what makes the system survive a vacation, a sick week, or a future hire. Do not skip this step. The system is not real until it is documented.

## Day 91 — the measurement

On day 91, open your book health sheet. Compare three numbers to your baseline from Module 1:

1. **Lapse count for the last 30 days** vs. same month last year. This is the headline metric.
2. **Save rate on Bucket 1 clients** — how many of the flagged at-risk clients retained. Healthy is above 50 percent.
3. **Hours spent on the ritual** — how many hours per week did you actually spend on retention work? Healthy is 3 to 6.

If lapses are down, save rate is above 50 percent, and hours spent are under 6 per week — the system is working. Keep going. Do not touch it. Do not add features. Do not try to make it fancier. Run it for another 90 days exactly as-is.

If lapses are flat — the system needs tuning, not replacement. Look at your draft quality (are the drafts actually going out or getting stuck in the approval queue), your Bucket 1 call follow-through (are the calls happening or slipping), and your voice samples (are they current). One of those three is almost always the culprit.

If lapses are up — do not panic. A single month is statistical noise. Wait for day 121 before drawing conclusions. If the trend holds at day 121, re-read Module 3 on segmentation and lapse signals and look for a systematic miss.

## What not to do in 90 days

Just as important as what to do, a list of what to skip:

- **Do not migrate AMS platforms.** The single most common rollout-killer. AMS migrations are 6-month projects that eat every bit of slack in your schedule.
- **Do not build custom software.** Whatever you would build, an off-the-shelf tool does 80 percent of it for 2 percent of the time.
- **Do not hire anyone.** Hire for the system once it is proven, not before.
- **Do not buy new tools beyond the two in Phase 1.** Adding a third tool in month two is the signal that you are avoiding the actual work.
- **Do not try to automate the save calls.** The calls are the point. Automating them is automating the wrong thing.
- **Do not try to measure ROI before day 91.** Early measurement is noise and the noise will discourage you.

The 90-day goal is not a finished, perfect system. The goal is proof that the system works so you can justify investing more time in the next 90 days. You are building a foundation, not a finished building.

## Do this today

Put the Week 1 setup block on your calendar right now. Three hours, named "Retention system Week 1 block." Protect it like a client meeting. If it is not on the calendar, it will not happen.

If you are already past Week 1 in your head, you are already behind the plan. The plan starts with one calendar block, not with reading ahead.
